# ethics

Systematic study of moral dilemmas

## Branches

### Virtue

Focus on rules and behavior that contribute to one's flourishing (reaching individual potential)

- Rooted in Aristotle's works
- Aristotle: the highest good is the state of "living well"

### Duty

Focus on obligations as moral agent, including intention
`Aka.` deontology

- Introduced by Christianity
	- Follow the rules because everyone is duty bound to obey God
	- Non believers know the rules without reading them
	- Non believers should still follow the rules
- Renaissance thinkers: maxims exist independent of God
- Advantages:
	- Simplicity
- Disadvantages:
	- Definition of maxims
	- Conflict between maxims

> [!abstract] maxim
> Universal moral rule

#### Ethical rationalism

Branch of deontology formalized by Kant

- Reason sets us free
	- => we're duty bound to follow reason
	- => action should reflect will
- **Imperative**: justification of the action (command)
	- Categorical: universal
		- The only categorical imperative: act only if the rule that applies to the action can be logically applied to everyone ("Act only on that maxim which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law")
		- Duty must be categorical to apply to everyone.
	- Hypothetical: conditional
- Rational beings have absolute worth => should not abuse each other
- Objects have conditional worth (dependent on will).

### Consequentialist

Focus on consequence instead of intention
`Aka.` teleology

- Refined by John Stuart Mill
- **Summum bonum**: end goal
- Hedonism: physical pleasure is the only good
- Mill: true happiness cannot be achieved by fixing on one's own happiness
- Disadvantages:
	- No consensus on the summum bonum
	- More complicated than expected
